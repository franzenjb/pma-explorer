# The Future Art Museum: A Working White Paper for the Portland Museum of Art

**Working title:** The Future Art Museum: From Collection Temple to Civic Operating System  
**Focus institution:** Portland Museum of Art, Portland, Maine  
**Working author:** Dragon Franzen  
**Status:** Draft 0.1 for discussion, revision, and eventual public white paper

---

## 1. Opening Claim

The Portland Museum of Art is not merely planning a larger museum. It is entering a once-in-a-generation decision point about what an art museum should become.

The proposed expansion has been described through the language of campus unification, public access, regional materials, sustainability, Wabanaki-informed design, and a more open relationship to the city. LEVER Architecture's concept describes a new ground-floor public passage, "Free Street," along with community lounge, maker spaces, performance space, and a multi-vocal relationship among making, performance, and exhibition. This is already more than an architectural project. It is a civic proposition.

The central question of this paper is:

> What is an art museum for in 2050, and is PMA building only more museum, or a new civic instrument for Portland and Maine?

This paper argues that the future art museum will succeed not by becoming louder, more digital, or more spectacular, but by becoming more useful: a civic learning platform where art, place, memory, technology, education, public life, and community belonging meet.

---

## 2. How the Art Museum Has Changed

### 50 years ago: the museum as temple

The late twentieth-century museum was largely collection-centered and expert-led. Authority flowed from curator to object to visitor. The dominant experience was quiet looking. The building itself often reinforced reverence: thresholds, stairs, galleries, labels, guards, and silence.

This model had strengths. It protected objects. It cultivated attention. It made a case for art as serious public inheritance.

But it also carried limits. It could feel exclusionary, class-coded, static, and indifferent to people who did not already know how to behave in a museum.

### 20 years ago: the museum as destination

By the early twenty-first century, art museums increasingly became destinations. Architecture, restaurants, cafés, shops, events, tourism, branding, and blockbuster exhibitions became central to the museum's public identity.

The museum was no longer only a keeper of collections. It was also a city asset, a hospitality venue, a donor platform, a weekend destination, and a cultural tourism anchor.

This expanded the audience, but it also introduced a risk: the museum could become an experience economy machine rather than a public learning institution.

### 10 years ago: the museum as platform

In the last decade, museums increasingly moved toward digital collections, participatory interpretation, community programming, inclusion work, family programs, social media, and more active educational models.

The visitor was no longer seen only as a passive recipient. Visitors became participants, learners, makers, critics, collaborators, and sometimes co-authors of meaning.

The museum also became more exposed to public expectations around access, equity, restitution, representation, labor, climate, community partnership, and institutional accountability.

### Now: the museum as contested civic infrastructure

The current museum is pulled in many directions. It is expected to be a place of contemplation, a tourist attraction, a community gathering place, a school partner, a climate-aware institution, a digital publisher, a collection steward, an employer, a public forum, a safe civic space, and a financial enterprise.

Recent American Alliance of Museums reporting shows that the field has recovered unevenly from the pandemic, with only about half of museums fully back to pre-pandemic attendance levels as of the 2024 national snapshot. The same field reports also point to major issues around attendance, staffing, philanthropy, digital vulnerability, volunteerism, public pressure, and institutional resilience.

The museum of the future must therefore answer a harder question than "how do we attract visitors?" It must answer: "why should people need us?"

## 3. What Might Change Next

### The next 10 years: the porous museum

In the next decade, the strongest museums will become more porous.

They will blur the boundary between museum and street. Their lobbies, courtyards, cafés, terraces, classrooms, and public programs will matter almost as much as their galleries. People will enter for many reasons: to see art, attend a talk, meet a friend, bring a child, study, rest, watch a film, hear music, take a class, or simply occupy a beautiful public room.

For PMA, this suggests that the proposed Free Street is not a minor amenity. It may be the heart of the future institution.

### The next 20 years: the museum as learning institution

Over twenty years, art museums may come to resemble hybrid institutions: part library, part university, part archive, part studio, part civic commons.

This does not mean abandoning art. It means interpreting art as a way into larger questions: place, history, climate, labor, identity, beauty, migration, memory, craft, technology, and ethical imagination.

A future PMA should not merely say, "Here is Winslow Homer." It should also ask: what does this work teach us about Maine, weather, risk, class, labor, coastlines, solitude, danger, and myth?

### The next 50 years: the museum as memory engine

Over fifty years, museums may become memory engines: institutions that preserve not only objects, but relationships among objects, people, stories, places, ecosystems, technologies, and communities.

The future museum may steward:

- physical works of art;
- digital art and born-digital archives;
- oral histories;
- Indigenous knowledge and protocols;
- community memory;
- artist process and studio records;
- climate and place-based interpretation;
- AI-assisted discovery tools;
- local civic history;
- and new forms of shared authorship.

The defining museum skill may shift from possession to interpretation, from collection to connection.

## 4. Lessons from Sister Institutions

### Libraries

Libraries are the most important sister institution for PMA to study.

Modern libraries are no longer only book warehouses. They are public workspaces, internet access points, children's learning centers, local archives, meeting spaces, civic information centers, technology labs, job search support centers, and trusted public rooms.

The lesson for museums is simple:

> Access beats reverence.

A future PMA should ask whether someone can use the museum even when they are not explicitly going to "see art."

Can they study there? Meet there? warm up there? bring a child there? hear music there? make something there? learn something there? return without buying a ticket every time?

### Educational institutions

Schools and universities have been moving away from pure lecture toward active learning, project-based learning, interdisciplinary work, digital tools, and lifelong education.

Museums can learn from this shift. The future museum should not only display finished things. It should expose process, inquiry, debate, ambiguity, and making.

A museum that teaches people how to look, compare, question, interpret, and make meaning is far more powerful than a museum that simply delivers labels.

### Public parks and civic spaces

Parks teach museums that civic value often comes from repeat, informal, low-friction use.

Many people use parks without a program, ticket, guide, or schedule. The future museum can learn from this. Not every visit must be event-driven. A great museum campus should allow ordinary use: wandering, resting, meeting, looking, thinking.

### Archives and local history organizations

Archives teach museums that memory requires structure. A museum that wants to be a civic memory engine must take seriously metadata, oral histories, local stories, artist records, community documentation, and long-term digital preservation.

### Performing arts centers

Performing arts centers teach museums that cultural buildings come alive through time-based programming. Music, performance, film, reading, dance, dialogue, and artist demonstration can make the museum active without turning it into entertainment.

PMA Films and performance space already point in this direction.

## 5. Implications for PMA

### 1. The expansion should be judged by use, not only beauty

The building may be beautiful, but beauty alone is not enough. The stronger question is: who uses it, how often, for what purposes, and with what sense of belonging?

### 2. Free Street should be treated as civic infrastructure

If Free Street is truly free, public, active, and welcoming, it could become one of Portland's most important indoor civic spaces.

Its success should be measured by public life, not only circulation.

### 3. The museum should support multiple modes of attention

The future PMA should allow for quiet looking, noisy making, study, conversation, performance, family activity, digital exploration, and public reflection. These modes need not compete if they are designed intelligently.

### 4. PMA should become a learning platform

The future museum should help visitors build interpretive skill. It should teach people how to look, compare, question, connect, and care.

### 5. Technology should deepen attention, not replace it

AI, digital collections, mobile interpretation, projection, audio, and interactive displays should not become gimmicks. The goal is not more screens. The goal is better looking, better context, better access, and better memory.

### 6. The museum should make Maine legible

PMA has a particular obligation and opportunity: to interpret Maine as a place of coastlines, forests, Wabanaki homelands, working waterfronts, tourism, climate vulnerability, craft, industry, migration, rural life, and artistic imagination.

This is not provincial. It is world-class because it is specific.

## 6. Core Questions for Discussion

1. Should PMA be primarily a museum, a civic commons, a learning institution, or all three?
2. Is the expansion solving yesterday's problem, which is space, or tomorrow's problem, which is relevance?
3. What parts of the museum should be free, always?
4. Can the museum serve people who do not already identify as museum people?
5. How should PMA balance contemplation with activity?
6. How can the museum serve Portland residents and not only tourists, donors, and art insiders?
7. What would make a teenager, retiree, teacher, artist, immigrant, family, or skeptical non-art person feel that the museum belongs to them?
8. What can PMA do that a website, school, library, theater, or entertainment venue cannot do alone?
9. How can Wabanaki knowledge be engaged with seriousness rather than used as design symbolism?
10. What should PMA refuse to become?

## 7. Working Framework: The Seven Futures of PMA

### 1. The Collection Museum

Preserves, studies, and displays art with rigor.

### 2. The Civic Commons

Offers public space, gathering, access, and belonging.

### 3. The Learning Museum

Teaches visual literacy, history, interpretation, and creative inquiry.

### 4. The Making Museum

Supports studios, workshops, artist process, and hands-on participation.

### 5. The Memory Museum

Connects art to Maine, community history, oral history, and place.

### 6. The Digital Museum

Publishes, interprets, personalizes, and preserves through digital tools without sacrificing embodied experience.

### 7. The Resilient Museum

Prepares for climate, financial, technological, political, and demographic change.

The strongest future PMA is not one of these. It is the disciplined combination of all seven.

---

## 8. Provocations

- PMA should not build a bigger museum. It should build Portland's cultural operating system.
- Free Street may matter more than any single gallery.
- The future museum is not quieter or louder. It is more plural.
- The most important visitor may be the person who does not yet think the museum is for them.
- Digital strategy should begin with memory, not marketing.
- Access is not a discount strategy. It is an institutional philosophy.
- A great museum does not merely preserve art. It trains a city to see.

## 9. Draft White Paper Structure

### Executive Summary

A concise statement of the argument, why PMA's expansion matters, and what choices are at stake.

### Section I: The Museum in Transition

A historical overview of how museums have moved from temples to destinations to platforms to civic infrastructure.

### Section II: PMA's Once-in-a-Generation Moment

A discussion of the expansion, Free Street, regional materials, Wabanaki-informed design, accessibility, and campus unification.

### Section III: Lessons from Sister Institutions

Libraries, schools, parks, archives, and performance spaces as models for the future museum.

### Section IV: The Seven Futures Framework

A strategic framework for evaluating museum choices.

### Section V: Risks

Risks include becoming too entertainment-driven, too donor-driven, too digitally shallow, too architecturally focused, too expensive to access, or too symbolic without operational change.

### Section VI: Recommendations

Potential recommendations:

1. Treat Free Street as a civic room, not a hallway.
2. Build learning into the museum's operating model.
3. Create a public digital collection and interpretation layer worthy of the building.
4. Design for repeat local use, not only special visits.
5. Establish clear community partnership protocols.
6. Make the museum a place for both contemplation and participation.
7. Measure success through belonging, learning, repeat use, and civic value.

### Section VII: Conclusion

The expansion is not only an architectural opportunity. It is a philosophical one. PMA can define a museum model rooted in Maine but relevant far beyond Maine.
